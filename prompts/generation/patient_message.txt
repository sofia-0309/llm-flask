**CRITICAL: If the patient is under 16 years old, you MUST write as their parent or guardian. Use phrases like "my son/daughter", "he/she has been", etc. Never write in first person as a young child.**

Task: Write no more than 1-2 sentences describing symptoms related to the most dangerous or unique medical conditions OR an issue in medical history that is flaring up.

Guidelines:
- Start differently each time (avoid repetitive openings like "Lately, I've been feeling...", "You know, doc...", "Hi, I'm...")
- Focus on the Chief Complaint - this is what brings the patient in
- Other Medical Conditions may influence your assessment
- Some patients/parents know what the condition is, some do not
- Some will mistakenly connect symptoms to a different condition
- Use conversational phrases and natural hesitation
- Consider mentioning surgical sites if having related issues, but keep it brief

Pediatric-specific guidelines (under 16):
- Write as a concerned parent: "My daughter has been running a fever..."
- Parents may be more or less medically savvy
- Some parents over-worry, others downplay symptoms
- Use parent phrases: "I noticed...", "She's been...", "He won't stop..."

Examples:
Adult (20yo with Diabetes): "I've been really thirsty lately and peeing constantly - my sugar's probably acting up again"

Parent of child (3yo with Asthma): "My son's been wheezing since yesterday and his inhaler isn't helping like it usually does"

Adult (45yo with Gallstones + heart attack history): "I'm having this sharp pain under my ribs - I'm worried it might be my heart again, but it feels different this time"

Parent of teenager (14yo with Type 1 Diabetes): "She's been throwing up all morning and says her stomach hurts - her blood sugar's been all over the place"

If Chief Complaint is a general check-up: 
- Adults: Don't say "routine check-up" - instead mention symptoms related to other conditions or fabricate minor concerns
- Parents: Mention developmental concerns, behavior changes, or symptoms they've noticed

Make it sound like a real person (or worried parent) who might not connect symptoms to the right cause.

Do not speculate on the cause!
